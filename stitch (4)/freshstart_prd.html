FreshStart — Product & Functionality Brief for Design What it is FreshStart is a web app for people who have just been released from incarceration in New York City. It meets them at one of the hardest moments of their life — the first days out — and gives them a clear, personalized plan for what to do next. It is not a directory, not a chatbot, and not a government portal. It is a guided roadmap that tells you specifically what to do, in what order, and exactly where to go. Who it's for The primary user is a returning citizen — someone just released from a city jail or state prison, returning to one of NYC's five boroughs. They may have been away for anywhere from a few months to over 20 years. Some will be comfortable with technology. Many will not — for some, this may be the first smartphone they've ever used. Some have been let down by institutions their entire life and will distrust anything that feels bureaucratic, cold, or official. The product must feel like a person helping them, not a system processing them. The problem it solves When someone is released, they are handed almost nothing. There are hundreds of nonprofits, city programs, and support organizations in NYC that exist to help returning citizens — but there is no single place that connects the dots. Nobody tells you: get your birth certificate first, then your Social Security card, then your NYC ID, and only then can you apply for benefits. Nobody tells you which shelter accepts people with records, or that one specific organization in the Bronx will give you a paid job the same day you walk in. People get lost. They miss critical windows. Some end up back inside because nobody helped them navigate the first 72 hours. FreshStart is that guide. How it works — the full user flow Step 1: Welcome The user lands on a screen that greets them warmly. There is a character (a mascot) that represents the app's voice — friendly, non-institutional, on their side. A short line of copy explains what the app does and reassures the user it will take only a few minutes. There is one button: get started. Step 2: Three questions The user answers three questions, one at a time, each on its own screen: Which borough are you returning to? — Bronx, Brooklyn, Manhattan, Queens, or Staten Island. This determines which resources are closest to them. How long were you away? — Options range from less than 1 year to 20+ years. This shapes how the plan is written — someone away for 20 years needs to know that smartphones, contactless payments, and the subway system have all changed. Someone away for 6 months needs different framing entirely. What is your most urgent need right now? — Four options: a place to stay, getting ID and benefits, finding work, mental health support. This determines what comes first in their roadmap. Each question is multiple choice. There is no typing required. The user taps an answer and moves to the next screen. Step 3: AI generates the roadmap After the three questions, the app sends the answers to an AI. The AI reads the answers, consults a curated database of real NYC organizations (addresses, phone numbers, hours), and builds a personalized plan. This takes a few seconds. A loading screen with the mascot reassures the user something is happening. Step 4: The roadmap The user sees their personalized roadmap — a list of 5 to 7 steps, ordered from most urgent to least. Each step tells them: What to do, in one clear sentence Why this step matters (one or two sentences, plain language, no jargon) Which category it belongs to: Housing, ID & Benefits, Employment, or Mental Health The steps are not generic. They are specific to the user's borough, their time away, and their stated need. Someone returning to the Bronx after 12 years who needs housing gets a different plan than someone returning to Brooklyn after 2 years who needs a job. Step 5: Resource details Each step has a "Show resource" action. When tapped, it reveals: The name of the specific organization The street address The phone number A short, practical tip for the visit (e.g. "Bring any document with your name on it — they work with what you have") All organizations are real, verified NYC nonprofits and city programs. The AI is constrained to only use organizations from a pre-approved list — it cannot make anything up. Step 6: Completion tracking Each step has a checkbox. As the user completes steps, they check them off. A progress bar at the top fills up. Completed steps are visually marked as done. This gives the user a sense of momentum — a feeling that they are moving forward, not drowning. Tone and voice The app speaks to the user the way a trusted friend who knows the system would speak — not the way a government form does. Sentences are short. Words are simple. Nothing assumes the user knows what "HRA" or "Medicaid" or "benefits enrollment" means without explaining it. The mascot's voice is warm, celebrates small wins, and never makes the user feel judged for not knowing something. What it does not do It does not require an account or login It does not ask for a name, case number, or any identifying information It does not store data between sessions It does not connect to any government system It does not provide legal advice It is not a hotline or crisis service (though it surfaces NYC Well, a 24/7 mental health line, as a resource) The four resource categories Every step in a roadmap belongs to one of these four lanes: Housing — finding a place to stay, transitional housing, emergency shelter options that accept people with records ID & Benefits — getting a birth certificate, Social Security card, and NYC ID (IDNYC, which is free and requires no prior ID to obtain), then applying for food stamps, Medicaid, and cash assistance Employment — organizations that hire returning citizens immediately, job training programs, resume help, and employers who do not disqualify for records Mental Health — free and low-cost counseling, peer support communities, crisis lines, and organizations that specialize in the emotional weight of reentry The core promise A person who has never heard of FreshStart, who picks up a phone 30 minutes after being released, should be able to open the app, answer three questions, and know exactly what to do next — with a real address to walk to and a real phone number to call.
